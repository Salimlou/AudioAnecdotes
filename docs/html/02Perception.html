Perceivable Auditory Latencies - DiFillipo and Greenebaum

These examples explore the perceptual effect of latency on different audio stimuli. Clickfusion and sidetone explore latency on perception. The McGurk effect video demonstrates how our perception deals with inconsistent audio and video stimulation.

 

Click Fusion

Run clickfusion to play a sequence of click pairs, each pair of clicks has ½ the time difference between them as the previous pair. Eventually, the first and second click can not be perceived as distinct, but they instead blend into a single sound.

 

To further explore click fusion space try running clickfusion hosted in the caliper adjustment tool:

 

caliper "clickfusion %f" –f 1 –m 2 -i 0.05

 

 

-Press space bar to play a click pair at the initial 50ms (5 hundredths of a second) delay.

-Press + a few of times to increase delay by 10ms each time until you can clearly distinguish two clicks

-Press – a bunch of times to decrease delay until you can not distinguish multiple clicks

-Press a digit (0-9) to change the magnitude of the difference if you want to further fine tune (for instance 3 will change the magnitude changed by +/- to 10^-3 or 0.001)

-At any time you can press i to return delay to the initial 50ms value

Sidetone

Run sidetone to experiment with how latency affects your ability to speak when your own voice is delayed and played back. This demo requires a microphone and works best with headphones to reduce feedback. Make sure that your computer is configured to accept audio input from the microphone and that the microphone is not in the output mix. For instance this may be adjusted on Window’s XP via:

 

Start->”All Programs”->Accessories->Entertainment->”Volume Control”

 

Options->Playback then mute the microphone from the “hotside” mix

Options->Recording the select the microphone and set the microphone level

 

(these operations are best achieved while running sidetone, removing the microphone from the hotside mix will eliminate being able to instantly hear microphone input in the headphones. Setting microphone recording levels will control the microphone input to the sidetone program which plays back microphone input after a delay.)

 

Once setup try counting, Zero, One, Two, Three... as an experiment. Many people find it difficult to perform even simple tasks such as counting when they hear their own voice repeated with sufficient amplitude and delay.

McGurk Effect

Play the McGurk_Effect video. In this video, a computer rendered face mouths one sound while a second (different) sound is played. For many people, the conflicting stimulation causes a third sound to be perceived. Try watching the video with the sound disabled or listening to the sound without watching the video.
